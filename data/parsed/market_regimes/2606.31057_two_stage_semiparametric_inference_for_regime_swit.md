## Abstract

We study high-frequency semiparametric inference for ergodic regime-switching jump diffusions whose continuous coefficients are parametric and whose regime-wise Lévy densities are unknown. The motivation is that jumps contaminate increments while their law is itself unknown, making likelihood-based inference circular in switching models. We propose a two-stage procedure. First, small increments are used in a truncated Gaussian quasi-likelihood to estimate the drift and diffusion parameters. Second, large drift-corrected residuals are sorted by regime and smoothed with a kernel, with normalization by empirical regime exposure time, to estimate the Lévy intensity densities on compact sets away from zero. We establish consistency and mixed-rate asymptotic normality for the quasi-maximum likelihood estimator, and derive L 2 ( B ) -convergence rates for the exposure-normalized residual density estimator. Simulations for switching Ornstein-Uhlenbeck models illustrate the finite-sample performance of the method.

Keywords: Markov switching diffusion, Lévy jumps, Gaussian quasi-likelihood, semiparametric inference, Lévy density estimation

Estimation of θ is related to parametric inference for discretely observed diffusions, which is well developed; see, for example, Kessler [11] and Gobet [10]. For models with Markovian switching, statistical inference for ergodic switching diffusions without jumps was developed by Cheng and Masuda [3], who used a Gaussian quasi-likelihood based on the observed regime path. That work is close to the switching component of the present paper, but the absence of jumps eliminates the thresholding problem and the infinite-dimensional nuisance component considered here. Regime-switching Lévy models have also been studied in financial econometrics by Chevallier and Goutte [4], but their setting is not the present high-frequency inference problem for a state-dependent switching jump diffusion with nonparametric regime-wise Lévy densities.

Estimation of s i is related to nonparametric Lévy-measure estimation from high-frequency data. Comte and Genon-Catalot [5] constructed estimators for Lévy processes over a long time interval and obtained L 2 -type risk bounds away from the origin, while Shimizu [21] studied density estimation for discretely observed diffusion processes with jumps. These approaches rely on the small-time approximation of the increment law by the Lévy measure, whose refinements were established by Figueroa-López [6] and Figueroa-López and Houdré [8]. Threshold methods for separating continuous and jump increments go back, in the present statistical context, to Mancini [15] and Shimizu and Yoshida [22]. The additional issue in our model is that the effective observation time is random and regime dependent; consequently, the normalization must be by regime exposure time rather than by the number of detected large increments.

To the best of our knowledge, these two directions have not been combined for discretely observed regimeswitching jump diffusions with unknown continuous coefficients and nonparametric regime-wise Lévy densities. The central idea of this paper is to separate the statistical roles of small and large increments. Small increments are used to estimate the drift and diffusion parameters through a truncated Gaussian quasilikelihood. The truncation removes the leading effect of large jumps, so that the unknown Lévy densities enter the first-stage procedure only as nuisance components. We prove consistency and asymptotic normality of the resulting Gaussian quasi-maximum likelihood estimator (GQMLE).

The large increments are then used to estimate the regime-wise Lévy densities. After the drift has been estimated, we form drift-corrected residuals, keep the residuals that pass a jump threshold, and sort them by the observed regime. The normalization is by the empirical exposure time in each regime, rather than by the number of detected jumps. This point is essential: a Lévy density is an intensity density, not the conditional distribution of a jump size given that a jump has occurred. The random exposure times determine the effective observation horizon in each regime and hence enter the stochastic part of the second-stage error.

The density estimator is studied on compact sets away from the origin. Its L 2 ( B ) -error has the standard bias-variance structure of a kernel estimator, together with remainders caused by discretization, thresholding, and the use of residuals. The second-stage result gives a direct convergence guarantee for the feasible residualbased estimator, with the regime exposure time playing the role of the effective observation horizon.

The nonparametric component is closest in spirit to the threshold kernel estimator of Shimizu [21], where large increments are used as proxies for unobserved jumps. Our purpose is not to improve the basic kernel rate. Rather, we adapt this idea to a semiparametric regime-switching jump diffusion in which the continuous dynamics are unknown and the effective observation horizon is regime dependent. The residual construction provides feasible jump proxies after the first-stage drift estimation, while the exposure normalization targets the regime-wise Lévy intensity density. The estimation is local in the jump size: on a compact set separated from the origin, only local smoothness of the Lévy density is needed. The framework therefore covers both finite-activity and infinite-activity jump mechanisms, allowing singular behavior near the origin, provided the density is smooth on the region where it is estimated.

The main technical contributions are therefore twofold. First, we establish the asymptotic theory of the truncated GQMLE in the presence of unknown regime-wise Lévy densities. Second, we derive L 2 ( B ) -rates for an exposure-normalized residual kernel estimator of each regime-wise Lévy density. These results combine threshold-based high-frequency estimation with regime-dependent exposure normalization and preliminary estimation of the continuous dynamics.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the model, observation scheme, notation and assumptions. Section 3 constructs the two estimators and states their asymptotic properties. Section 4 reports simulation results. Section 5 contains the proofs.

## 3. Two-stage estimation procedure

The presence of both a diffusive and a jump component in (2.2) suggests a two-stage strategy. Small increments of X are governed by the continuous martingale and drift parts and carry information about the parametric pair θ = ( α, γ ) ; large increments are essentially generated by single jumps and carry information about the regime-wise Lévy densities s ⋆ i , i ∈ S . Accordingly:

- Stage 1. estimate θ = ( α, γ ) by a truncated Gaussian quasi-maximum likelihood method based on the small increments of X ;
- Stage 2. use the resulting ˆ θ n = (ˆ α n , ˆ γ n ) to form drift-corrected residuals from the large increments and recover s ⋆ i by kernel smoothing.

Section 3.1 constructs the two estimators; Section 3.2 states their asymptotic properties. All proofs are deferred to Section 5.
