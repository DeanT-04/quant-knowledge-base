## The Relationship between Information Processing and Meaning Generation among Heterogeneous groups of Investors and the Market Asset Price Movement

Inga Ivanova, 1

## Abstract

The paper examines how the market asset's price movement emerges from the information exchange between heterogeneous groups of traders. In this respect financial markets, as complex social systems, are examined from the perspective of communication theory regarding the dynamics of information and meaning. The paper also contributes to the development of the quantity theory of meaning as applied to financial markets. The model is based on the quantitative theory of meaning, which can be considered as a complement to quantitative theory of information. Different groups of traders use different criteria for processing information, so the same information can have different meanings. Meanings shape traders' expectations, which are manifested in the price movements of market assets. These dynamics can be captured using a nonlinear evolutionary equation. Model predictions are tested on a variety of foreign exchange, energy, food and indices markets over different time periods. The results show that the time series predicted by the model are co-integrated with the time series of market asset prices, suggesting that the proposed model can adequately describe some traders' behavior patterns. In a narrow sense the proposed methodology can be used to better understand and predict future price movements of market assets. I a wider sense it helps to better understand the behavior of heterogeneous social systems of different origins since information is a universal concept. From this point of view, the transmission of information in financial markets is not much different from the transmission of information in other social systems. The same conceptual tools can be applied to study other complex social systems with similar topologies.

## I. Introduction

The question of whether financial markets can be predicted is a major concern for traders and policymakers. The same applies to mechanisms to control price movements of market assets. A quick glance at the price charts of some market assets reveals periods of directional price movement and periods of price swings that reflect traders' decisions on whether to buy, sell, or exit the market. Traders are guided by logic and psychology in their decisions. The delicate balance between these two factors determines traders' preferences, which guide their decisions. Preferences, in turn, are shaped by market information, which is either analyzed rationally or distorted by psychological biases. This analysis provides meaning to the information that serves as a basis for further action. The purpose of the paper is to study the mechanisms that control traders' preferences, the formation of financial time series and the ability to predict future movements in asset prices. There are various approaches to studying market dynamics in order to predict future changes. One idea behind estimating the price movements of market assets is that past price values can indicate their future values according to observed market trends. It is the cornerstone of technical analysis (Miner, 2002). Elliott wave patterns (Elliott, 1994) and cycles, some of which may be related to business cycles, are used in market timing strategy (Millard, 1999).

Another approach argues that future price values have no relation to past price values, so asset prices resemble the movement of molecules (Osborn, 1959; Osborn and Murphy, 1984, cf. Fama, 1965; Malkiel, 1973). The efficient market hypothesis (EMH) (Fama, 1970) and related theories (e.g. Black and Scholes, 1973; Merton, 1973; Gulko, 1997, etc.) assume that asset prices reflect all available information, so future prices can not be predicted. However, there are also examples of predictable price behavior (for example, Working, 1960; Cowles and Jones, 1937; Kendall, 1953), which are considered anomalous under the random walk hypothesis. In addition, machine learning-based research has been reported to provide high forecasting accuracy for financial time series (e.g., Chang et al ., 2009; Bitwai and Cohn, 2014; Patel et al ., 2015; Hsua et al ., 2016).

Behavioral economics, which focuses on the various psychological factors that influence people's economic decisions, takes a different approach from EMH (e.g., Kahneman and Tversky, 1979; Thaler, 1980, 1985; Banerjee, 1992). It states that the decisions of economic agents can largely be considered irrational and driven by psychological factors, so the hypothesis of rational agents can no longer be supported. A number of publications examine the mechanisms that determine traders decisions in financial markets (e.g. Shiller, 1981; Statman, 1995; Olsen, 1998; Barber and Odean, 1999). Because deviations from full rationality are systematic and can be modeled and studied, knowledge of these mechanisms can be used to improve predictions of future traders' behavior and market asset price movements. For example, in the case of herd behavior, market trends that have formed to date can be extrapolated to some future period. This indicates that the market is somewhat predictable and past prices can sometimes be used to predict the direction of future price changes (Lo and Mackinlay, 2002). The adaptive market hypothesis (Lo, 2004, 2005) views the EMH and the behavioral approach as opposite sides of the same coin, interpreting the behavioral biases of market participants in an evolutionary perspective, as adaptation to changing conditions of the market environment, so that each group of market participants behaves accordingly. In its evolutionary aspect, the adaptive market hypothesis is close to the complex systems approach, which considers the market as a complex developing system of connected networks of interacting agents. Complexity theory, as applied to financial markets, examines nonlinearity, self-organization, emergent dynamics, and develops predictive models such as stock market crashes (Sornette, 2003). While behavioral finance primarily focuses on individual-level biases, social finance-a new paradigm in financial research-focuses on cultural characteristics, including information signals, beliefs, strategies, etc., adopted by larger groups of investors. 'Analysis of social interactions promises to provide greater insight into where heuristics come from, and to offer a foundation for understanding shifts in investor sentiment. As such, it can potentially offer a deeper basis for understanding the causes and consequences of financial bubbles and crises' (Hirshleifer, 2015, p. 44).

A common assumption in the approaches mentioned is that market asset prices reflect investors' reactions to information. But it is necessary to ask questions: 1) how investors process information; 2) whether the same information evokes the same meaning among different groups of investors; 3) does the way investors process information play a more significant role than the information itself? The purpose of the article is to find answers to these questions.

The paper proposes a new approach based on the processing of information by heterogeneous groups of investors that determines their behavior. Upon receipt, the information is processed, i.e. supplied with meaning. Different groups of investors may process the same information differently, providing different criteria by which to make sense of the information. These criteria can be defined as the selection environments in terms of specific coding rules (or sets of communication codes). Coding rules govern latent structures that organize different meanings into structural components (Leydesdorff, 2010). 'Meanings originate from communications and feedback on communications. When selections can operate upon one another, a complex and potentially non-linear dynamics is generated' (Leydesdorff, 2021, p. 15). Meanings give rise to expectations regarding possible states of the system, which are generated with respect to the future. Expectations operate as feedback on the current state (i.e. against the arrow of time). In other words, the system simultaneously takes into account its past, present and future state, in line with Bachelier's observation that 'past, present and even discounted future events are reflected in the market price' (Bachelier, 1964). Expectations provide a source of additional options for possible future states of the system that are available but not yet realized.

The more options a system has, the greater the likelihood that the system will deviate from its previous state in the process of autocatalytic self-organization. A measure of additional capacity is redundancy, which is defined as the addition of information to its maximum informational content (Brooks and Wiley, 1986). Regarding innovation studies, redundancy is associated with synergy in the Triple Helix (TH) model of university-industry-government relations (e.g. Etzkowitz and Leydesdorff, 1995, 1998; Leydesdorff, 2003; Park and Leydesdorff, 2010; Leydesdorff and Strand, 2013, etc.). The interaction between differently shaped traders' expectations can ultimately lead to nonlinear market price dynamics.

The paper's contribution is two-fold: 1) in a narrow sense, it presents a model of market dynamics that sheds light on the mechanisms that govern the formation of prices of market assets and, in some cases, allows us to predict future price movements; 2) in a broader sense, it is a step forward in the development of a theory of meaning, which brings this theory into a wider practical area. The first research objective of this paper is to test the applicability of the general concept of information and meaning to describe market price dynamics. The second goal of the study is to provide a quantitative description of market price movements based on the evolutionary dynamics of traders' expectations. This dynamics is captured by a nonlinear evolutionary differential equation, the solutions of which provide recognizable patterns. One can use these patterns to predict future asset price movements, which allows make more accurate forecasts of future price changes.

## II. Literature review

The approach that uses meaning generation in inter-social communications is not rooted in financial literature. However, models with similar basic characteristics can be found in the literature. Financial time series modeling has been a subject of debate among scholars and practitioners. A big branch of the literature is devoted to machine forecasting of time series. Related approaches include traditional Machine Learning (ML) models (e.g. Bahrammirzaee, 2010; Mullainathan, S. and Spiess, 2017, etc.) and emerging within the ML field Deep Learning (DL) models such as Artificial Neural Networks (ANN ), Recurrent Neural Network (RNN), Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), and Deep Multilayer Perceptron (DMLP) (e.g., Schmidhuber, 2015; Sokolov et al ., 2020). The advantage of the analytical approach compared to the numerical one is that in analytical models it is possible to trace the properties of time series depending on the behavior of investors. Frequently observed and difficult to explain facts such as excess volatility (LeRoy &amp; Porter, 1981; Shiller, 1981), volatility clustering (Mandelbrot, 1997), etc., have led to a growing body of literature on heterogeneous agent based models (HAM) (e.g. Hommes, 2006, Chiarella, Dieci and He, 2009), in which the financial market includes different groups of agents. Agent interactions can generate complex market dynamics involving both chaos and stability (e.g., Brock and Hommes, 1997; Lux, 1995; Bonabeau, 2002). Kaizoji (2004) showed that periodic chaos in asset price dynamics can be observed in a simple model of financial markets with two groups of agents, which can be explained by the heterogeneity of traders' trading strategies. The behavior of financial markets during periods of crisis can be described as rogue waves (e.g., Jenks, 2020). This type of waves is analytically depicted in a nonlinear option pricing model (Yan, 2010) and can be used to describe the possible mechanisms of the rogue wave phenomenon in financial markets. Rogue waves can be detected in Korteweg de Vries (KdV) systems when real nonintegrable effects, higher order nonlinearity, and nonlinear diffusion are taken into account (Lou and Lin 2018). Dhesi and Ausloos (2016), while studying the behavior of agents reacting to time-dependent on the log returns within an Irrational Fractional Brownian motion model, observed a kink-like effect reminiscent of soliton behavior. Next, they posed the question: what is the differential equation whose solution describes this effect? In some ways, the idea behind this approach is similar to that used in opinion dynamics models (e.g., Zha et al ., 2020; Granha et al ., 2022). Binge and Boshoff (2021) introduced a pseudo-resales method for constructing art prices. They proposed a regression-based method that provides a more accurate estimate of price changes and allows the study of bubbles in a range of asset markets. The gap between an asset's price and fundamental value will exhibit temporary explosive behavior. The resulting bubble is expected to eventually burst and hence a trend reversal can be predicted. In the absence of structural changes in fundamentals, the period of explosive prices has a non-fundamental explanation, namely cognitive bias. The inability of traders to properly process available information can cause systematic patterns in price movements, such as seasonality. This inability can be explained by a cognitive bias on the part of investors (Hirshleifer et al ., 2020; Fang et al ., 2021), who use ease-of processing heuristics when processing information relevant to the pricing of market assets. Knowledge of seasonality mechanisms allows us to predict the dynamics of repo rates. Biased beliefs about future price movements are an important driver of market prices. Changes in traders' expectations about future stock market returns can explain facts about stock market price movements (De la O and Myers, 2021). Jin and Sui (2022) showed that investors' beliefs about future market returns can cause excessive volatility in stock market returns. These beliefs depend primarily on recent past returns and can provide significant returns predictability. The behavior of a group of traders in interpreting external information also influences the movement of stock prices. Massa et al . (2022) showed that the reallocation of strategic risks of business groups in response to news that can be interpreted as 'bad' information is an additional factor in shaping stock returns. Duffy et al . (2021) studied how exchange traded funds (ETFs) affect asset prices and turnover in a laboratory asset market consisting of three subgroups of traders, with each subgroup employing its own preferential trading strategy. The three-part market structure is also described within the SIR model, which is used to explain the spread of investor ideas and bubbles (Shiller, 2019). SIR-related models can explain price jumps, bubbles, and the serial correlation structure in asset returns with different lags (Burnside et al ., 2016; Hirshleifer, 2020). The SIR model, in turn, can be coupled with the TH model and reveal the corresponding nonlinear dynamics described by the KdV equation (Ivanova, 2022). Noussair and Popescu (2021) provide evidence that market asset co-movements in the absence of a common fundamentals shock can be explained by behavioral factors driven by asymmetric information between informed and uninformed traders. A new paradigm for understanding financial markets is social finance (Akçay and Hirshleifer, 2021). Social finance argues that the behavior of market participants depends on the accepted cultural characteristics that determine the operating patterns of markets. These traits are common to larger investor groups and provide bias in the transmission of information between investor groups. Investors' financial characteristics are volatile and may change over time.

## III. Method

The paper's method draws on the general theory of meaning in inter-social communications (Leydesdorff, 2021), which is based on an information-theoretic approach to measuring synergy in the Triple Helix model of innovation.The market is a complex social system comprised of various groups of traders - pension funds, banks, hedge funds, individuals, etc. whose compound dynamics drive market price movement. Traders behave in accordance with their investment preferences. Instead of using an approach to market microstructure that relies on computational agent-based models that simulate the behavior of individual traders (e.g., O'Hara, 1995), one can focus on information processing among larger groups of investors.With respect to traders' sentiments they can be subdivided into momentum, reversal, and interactive traders. Traders make their decisions based on communicated information. However this information should first be given meaning (as a 'signal') or discarded (as noise). Each group uses different criteria for filtering information in accordance with their behavioral biases. That is the same information is considered from different perspectives (or positions), and can be supplied with different meanings. Mechanisms of information and meaning processing are different. Whereas information can be communicated via a network of relationships meanings are provided from different positions (Burt, 1982). Meaning cannot be communicated but only shared when the positions overlap. The processing of meaning can enlarge or reduce the number of options that can be measured as redundancy (Leydesdorff and Ivanova, 2014). The calculus of redundancy is complementary to calculus of information. ## IV. Data

The paper relies on publicly available data sets. The data comprise Australian dollar daily data (2001.09.21 -2004.05.13), Swiss franc hourly data (2021.01.07 - 2021.04.12), natural gas daily data (2016.03.02 - 2017.02.13), Corn daily data (2020.08.10 -2021.07.23), and S&amp;P500 index monthly data (2009.02.01 - 2022.01.01). Various data sets are specially chosen in order to test the model applicability across different markets and different time scales. I compare the model results with the empirically observed data on FX, energy, food, and indices markets. Since the S&amp;P 500 index market data take a fairly long time interval I use inflation-adjusted data.

## V. Results

It follows from the model that when non-linear effects prevail (Equation A 10) asset prices develop according to trends that can be described by the non-linear evolutionary equation (7). In other cases, there may be other mechanisms that determine price dynamics.

To answer the question of whether the concept of information and meaning communication can be applied to describe the dynamics of market prices, one can try to find patterns that can be described by the model. I compare the model's results with empirically observed data from foreign exchange, energy, food, and indices markets. The same patterns can be found across different markets and (due to the fractal nature of stock and financial markets) across different time frames. The selection of different assets from different markets (FX, energy, food, and indices) is made to illustrate the application of the model to different markets. Because market data for the S&amp;P 500 index takes a fairly long time frame I used inflation-adjusted data. ## VI. Discussion

The results show that the model can accurately describe certain patterns on price charts, such as trends, and can indicate whether a trend is likely to continue or terminate. The model can also be applied to predict not only future asset prices, but also economic crises. For example Standard and Poor's 500 stock market index includes the 500 largest companies whose shares are traded on US stock exchanges. This is one of the largest stock indices which largely reflects the state of the US economy (and the world economy). A model predicting the end of the third wave of a historical trend could indicate that we are on the cusp of the next global recession. It is also possible to test the behaviour of market asset prices during periods of price consolidation since Equation 7 also has periodic solutions in the form of cnoidal waves, but due to the paper volume limitations, it remains a topic of future research. In this paper a non-linear differential equation is considered as a basic technique for describing price dynamics. Differential equations were previously used by Caginalp &amp; Balenovich to provide foundation to the technical analysis. The authors noted that 'While economic and financial scholars often ignore or downplay the role of conventional technical analysis, financial experts are often quite eager to implement it without regard to the nature of the economic assumptions inherent in the methods' (Caginalp &amp; Balenovich, 2003, p.7). They showed that some technical analysis patterns can be modelled using differential equations by assuming interactions between two or more groups of traders with different valuations and/or different motivational characteristics. However, the reasons behind the investor sentiment were not given.

The information approach allows us to establish another connection with technical analysis. This refers to Fibonacci retracement, which is used in technical trend analysis of financial market trends to predict price support and resistance levels. Fibonacci numbers are a sequence in which each subsequent number is the sum of the two previous numbers: 0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21 … One can construct the ratios of these numbers and mirror ratios. The most commonly used values are: 0.382; 0.500; 0.618; 1; 1.618; 2.618; 4.236. These ratios are often used in Fibonacci retracement to determine support and resistant levels and compare market price movements to each other (e.g. Colby, 2003). In an attempt to describe time evolution of the market, Miner (2002) used Fibonacci ratios to determine market time cycles. One common approach is to take past waves and use Fibonacci ratios to predict the relationship of past waves to future waves. For example using alternate price projection (APP) one can forecast the proportion of a past wave to the next wave, moving in the same direction. The most important comparison ratios for such swings that are considered to have the highest likelihood of supporting or breaking a trend are: 62%, 100%, 162%, 200%, 262%, and 424%. Another method involves the use of price expansion (Exp), which expands the price range of a wave. The same the most important ratios to use for price expansions are: 62%, 100%, 162%, 200%, 262%, and 424% (Miner, 2002). The reason which may make traders feel uncomfortable is that Fibonacci retracement is not grounded in any logical proof and there is no rational basis for this approach. The information approach may provide possible explanation for the origin of Fibonacci relationships. Table 5 lists the first seven Fibonacci ratios and the corresponding soliton amplitudes obtained from Equation 8. From Table 5 it appears that the Fibonacci ratios correspond relatively well to the soliton amplitudes with a maximum relative difference of 12%.

The presented model can be used to predict trend reversals. At the very beginning, it is impossible to say for sure that a trend price movement will begin to unfold. But when the trend unfolds and wave structure is confirmed, one can expect to reach certain price levels at certain points in time. If the level is reached by the planned time point, a (temporary) trend reversal can be expected. However, the presented model should not be considered as a universal tool for forecasting financial time series in any periods of trend and price consolidation, since equation 7 was obtained under certain conditions that cannot always be met.

At the same time, the information approach contributes to the growing literature examining the role of social norms, moral attitudes, religions, and ideologies in the imperfect rationality of market participants. Recently, there has been a growing understanding that investors' thoughts and behavior are influenced by accepted cultural traits, which can be viewed as an evolutionary system. This forms the basis of a new paradigm for understanding financial markets - social finance (e.g. Akçay, Hirshleifer, 2020). Like social finance, the information approach complements the study of how social interaction shapes the thinking and behavior of market participants. Both social finance and information approaches consider group characteristics of agents as the main cause of social transmission biases at different time scales. These features develop in the process of communication. There are also differences between the two approaches: 1) social finance is explicitly focused on how social interaction shapes the thinking and behavior of traders, the information approach goes beyond the field of finance and can be applied to the broader field of inter-social communications. (innovation research, rumors propagation, etc.); 2) cultural traits cannot be measured directly, whereas meaning can be measured using redundancy within the information approach; 3) social finance and information approach use different mathematical apparatus. The introduction of the method of nonlinear evolutionary equations allows us to adequately describe some observed price patterns and additionally provide a basis for technical analysis, including Fibonacci retracement.

## VI. Conclusion

This paper presents the first study of the dynamics of market price dynamics which examines the role of information processing and meaning generation in social systems. The study builds on the Loet Leydesdorff's seminal works on the dynamics of expectations and meaning in inter-human communications (Leydesdorff, 2008; Leydesdorff &amp; Dubois, 2004; Leydesdorff, Dolfsma, Van der Panne, 2006; Leydesdorff &amp; Franse, 2009; Leydesdorff &amp; Ivanova, 2014; Leydesdorff, Petersen &amp; Ivanova, 2017). It also incorporates the conceptual framework of the Triple Helix model of university-industry-government relations (Etzkowitz &amp; Leydesdorff, 1995, 1998) and its mathematical formulation (Ivanova &amp; Leydesdorff, 2014a, 2014b).

The main contribution of the paper is that it represents a step forward in the development of a theory of meaning that has potentially has many more arears of applicability than financial research. In relation to the study of stock and financial markets market assets' price movement is analysed from the viewpoint of information and meaning dynamics among market participants. This paper can also be considered an example of the application of the theory of meaning, which has long been considered an abstract discipline, to the specific field of financial markets. Another contribution is the introduction of a quantitative model to study the dynamics of market asset price movements and predict future price movements, based on a non-linear evolutionary equation.

Meaning in social communications is processed via specific sets of communication codes that span horizons of meaning, acting as selection and coordination mechanisms. It is based on expectations and caused by events against the arrow of time. Expectations can be measured as redundancy (i.e. additional options) using Shannon's entropy information theory. predict events that have not yet occurred. It can be useful when applied to various fields related to information exchange in social systems in general and to behavioral economics and financial

Conceptually, there is much to be gained from combining and testing these ideas with new methods and approaches. Perhaps even more important is the idea that meaning can be quantified. This is a very interesting and promising research area that has great potential to expand our ability to understanding the dynamics of social systems and improve our ability to markets in particular. The findings of the paper may also be useful for market practitioners in their daily activities.

The main provisions of the paper are as follows: the market can be considered as an ecosystem with bi- and trilateral relations between agents. In this respect mechanisms driving market evolution are similar to those of the TH model of innovations. Asset price dynamics can be analyzed from an information theory perspective, taking into account the relationships between information processing and meaning generation. Agents represent three groups of traders, which can be considered distributions spanning a network of relations through which information is communicated. There are other dynamics on the top of this network. Different agents use different communication codes, reflecting their preferences, to assign meaning to the information. Codes can be considered eigenvectors in a vector-space (von Foerster, 1960) and structure communications as selection environments. Communicated information is supplied with different meaning by different agents. Meaning is provided from the perspective of hindsight. Meanings cannot be communicated, as in case of information, but only shared. Providing information with meaning increases the number of options (redundancy). This mechanism can be considered probabilistically using Shannon's equations (Shannon, 1948). The generation of options (redundancy) is crucial for system change. The trade-off between the evolutionary generation of redundancy and the historical variation that provides uncertainty can be measured as negative and positive information, respectively. The dynamics of information, meaning, and redundancy can be evaluated empirically using the sign of mutual information as an indicator. When the dynamics of expectations, that generates redundancy, prevail over the historical construction that generates entropy, mutual redundancy is negatively signed because the relative uncertainty is reduced by increasing the redundancy. The balance between redundancy and entropy can be depicted in terms of two vectors ( P and Q ) which can also be understood in terms of the generation and reduction of uncertainty in communication resulting from the interactions between three (bi-lateral) communication channels. Eventually non-linear mechanisms in redundancy evolution may prevail leading to the predictable behavior in the evolution of market prices.  This does not mean that market price movements are completely predictable, but during certain periods a plausible estimate of price changes can be made.

The approach entertained in this paper considers the market an evolving social system and follows Luhmann's conjecture (Luhmann, 1982) that evolution theory, systems theory, and communication theory can be programmatically integrated from a sociological perspective . The  significance  of  paper  findings  is  due  not  only  to  accurate  description  of  some  observed market  phenomena  but  also  to  the  connection  with  technical  analysis  and  social  finance.  The noted connections of information approach with various arears of financial research and accepted practice  of  market  practitioners  give  hope  for  the  future  unification  of  various  approaches  in market research. Future research includes applying the present approach to some other problems and datasets with different numbers of features.
